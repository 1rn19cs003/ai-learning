LLM & RAG

The complete track: how large language models work, and how to build retrieval systems around them — at the depth a MAANG interview demands.

The promise of this track: finish all 12 chapters and their labs and you will be able to derive attention on a whiteboard, explain why your RAG pipeline failed and which stage caused it, implement a tool-calling agent from scratch, and defend every architectural decision with numbers. Every chapter has worked math, from-scratch TypeScript, corrected misconceptions, and interview questions with strong answers.
Part I — How LLMs actually work
CHAPTER 01

The math that runs everything

Vectors, dot products, matrices, softmax, gradients, backprop — with a two-layer network trained by hand.

 CHAPTER 02

Tokens & embeddings

BPE built step-by-step on a toy corpus, why token costs work the way they do, and the geometry of meaning.

 CHAPTER 03

The transformer, derived

Attention computed by hand with real numbers, multi-head, RoPE, the residual stream, and KV-cache memory math.

 CHAPTER 04

How models are trained

Pretraining, scaling laws, SFT, RLHF vs DPO, and LoRA with the parameter math that makes it 1000× cheaper.
Part II — Using LLMs like an engineer
CHAPTER 05

LLM engineering

Sampling math, prompting that survives production, structured outputs, tool calling, streaming, cost budgets.

 CHAPTER 06

Vector search internals

HNSW walked layer by layer, IVF/PQ, the filtered-search trap, and when Postgres is all you need.
Part III — RAG, naive to state of the art
CHAPTER 07

RAG fundamentals

The full pipeline in code, chunking with real numbers, and the failure taxonomy that drives everything after.

 CHAPTER 08

Advanced RAG

BM25 term by term, RRF worked example, cross-encoders, HyDE, contextual retrieval, Graph RAG, CRAG/Self-RAG.
Part IV — Evals, agents, production
CHAPTER 09

Evaluation

The RAG triad, Ragas metrics with their formulas, LLM-judge pitfalls, golden sets, and evals as CI gates.

 CHAPTER 10

Agents & orchestration

A ReAct agent in ~80 lines of real TypeScript, routing, critic loops, and when NOT to use agents.

 CHAPTER 11

Production engineering

Prompt caching economics, model routing, semantic caching, prompt injection defense, monitoring with eval canaries.

 CHAPTER 12

RepoSage — the flagship build

The full multi-agent codebase intelligence engine: components, contracts, build plan, and how to present it in interviews.
